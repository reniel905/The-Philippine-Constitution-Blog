The Philippine Constitution

A blog written by Group 4 & 5

December 10, 2023

The Philippine Constitution, adopted on February 2, 1987, is a crucial document in the Philippine history, it represents the culmination of efforts to restore democracy after years of authoritarian rule under President Ferdinand Marcos. The previous constitution, the 1973 Constitution, had been in effect during the martial law era, and the push for a new constitution emerged as a response to the desire for political reform and the restoration of democratic principles. The 1987 Constitution was the result of a Constitutional Commission appointed by then-President Corazon Aquino, who assumed the office after the People Power Revolution of 1986. The commission, composed of representatives from various sectors, worked to draft a constitution that would embody the values of democracy, human rights, and social justice. The process was characterized by a commitment to inclusivity and a recognition of the need to address historical injustices.

What are the constitutions that the Philippines had?

Malolos Constitution 1899 +

It was the first constitution of the Philippines and the first republican constitution in Asia. It was drafted in Malolos. Bulacan by the Malolos congress was approved on January 21, 1899. The constitution was kind of like a contract between the government and the people, saying what the government could and could not do, and how the people would choose their leaders. It was a big deal since this was the first time the Philippines had its own constitution, making it a truly independent nation.

The 1935 Constitution +

It served the Philippines well for many years, but it was eventually replaced with a new one in 1973. However, it remains an important part of Philippine history and a reminder of the country’s journey to independence and self-government. The 1935 Constitution was like a big rule book for the Philippines before they got full independence from the US. It was written by Filipinos to set up a government that would work for everyone and help the country grow. It is based on American laws, but it had some unique trait that were important to the Filipino people.

The 1973 Constitution +

Made and ratified by the Filipinos during the Marcos Period. The 1973 Constitution established the Philippines as democratic and republican state, with a presidential system of government. It provided for a separation of powers between the legislative, executive, and judicial branches of government, and it guaranteed certain fundamental rights and freedoms to all citizens, such as freedom of speech, religion, and the press. In addition to its provisions for government structure, the 1973 Constitution also included a bill of rights that guaranteed certain fundamental rights and freedoms to all citizens. It also provided for the protection of the rights of indigenous peoples, women, and children. Overall, the 1973 Constitution of the Philippines was a significant document that established the country’s democratic system of government and guaranteed certain fundamental rights and freedoms to all citizens. It has been amended several times over the years to reflect the changing needs and aspirations of the Filipino people.

The 1987 Constitution +

The 1987 Constitution of the Philippines, ratified on February 2, 1987, replaced the 1973 Constitution and embodies democratic principles, human rights, and the rule of law. Key features include a preamble expressing the Filipino people’s ideals, a comprehensive Bill of Rights, and the establishment of a democratic system with a separation of powers among the executive, legislative, and judicial branches. The Philippine Congress is bicameral, consisting of the Senate and the House of Representatives. The President serves a single six-year term, elected through a popular vote. The constitution ensures an independent judiciary, local autonomy, economic provisions safeguarding national interests, and establishes Constitutional Commissions like COMELEC, COA, and CSC. It reflects a response to the authoritarian rule of Ferdinand Marcos, emphasizing democratic values and social justice. Amendments can be made through a people’s initiative, constitutional convention, or by Congress as a constituent assembly.
